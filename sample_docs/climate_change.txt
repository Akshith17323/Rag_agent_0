Climate change is a pressing global crisis characterized by long-term shifts in temperatures and weather patterns. While natural variations exist, human activities since the 1800s have been the primary driver of climate change, primarily due to the burning of fossil fuels like coal, oil, and gas. Burning fossil fuels generates greenhouse gas emissions that act like a blanket wrapped around the Earth, trapping the sun's heat and raising temperatures. The main greenhouse gases that are causing climate change include carbon dioxide and methane. These come from using gasoline for driving a car or coal for heating a building, for example. Clearing land and forests can also release carbon dioxide. Landfills for garbage are a major source of methane emissions. Energy, industry, transport, buildings, agriculture, and land use are among the main emitters.

The effects of climate change are widespread and severe. Rising global temperatures lead to shrinking ice sheets, retreating glaciers, and decreasing snow cover, which collectively contribute to rising sea levels. This elevation in sea level poses a significant threat to coastal communities, increasing the risk of flooding and erosion. Furthermore, climate change intensifies extreme weather events, making hurricanes, droughts, and heatwaves more frequent and severe. These changes disrupt ecosystems, threatening biodiversity and altering agricultural yields, which can lead to food insecurity. The acidification of oceans, caused by the absorption of excess carbon dioxide, further endangers marine life, particularly coral reefs. Addressing climate change requires urgent global cooperation to transition to renewable energy sources, enhance energy efficiency, and protect natural carbon sinks like forests and oceans.
